Case files

Delivery cases, in STAR format.

Five public-safe cases from enterprise delivery, each told as Situation, Task, Action, and Result. Client-sensitive details are generalized; the management pattern is preserved.

01 — Recovery

Troubled projects moved from escalation to controlled delivery.

Situation
Two inherited projects at a retail client carried unclear requirements, missed milestones, formal complaints, and penalty pressure. The team was reacting to incoming requests without a stable scope baseline.

Task
Take ownership of both streams and return them to controlled, predictable delivery — without losing the client or triggering penalties.

Action
Rebuilt the factual picture of delivered and open work, aligned the remaining scope with the client, created a three-month recovery roadmap, and introduced one board with owners, dates, and a meeting rhythm.

Result
Escalation stopped, communication returned to a working mode, the recovery roadmap was delivered on the agreed dates, and the same client later expanded the relationship.

02 — Portfolio

Up to twelve parallel initiatives across clients and teams.

Situation
After a large project, the portfolio grew step by step — two projects for a retail client, a new client won, then two inherited problem streams — until it peaked at twelve parallel initiatives across two clients and three teams, with overlapping resources and competing commercial priorities.

Task
Keep the whole portfolio governable: synchronize client and team expectations, protect the timeline and budget of every stream, and build durable client communication.

Action
Ran the portfolio on a shared system — prioritization, SLAs, one task tracker, and, above all, transparency. Set a delivery rhythm for new and at-risk streams (daily, then weekly) and worked with clients directly on priorities and roadmap.

Result
Ten of twelve initiatives delivered on their original dates; every initiative launched from scratch under my management was delivered on time. Over the period the portfolio generated nine new orders across HR and EHS streams, and the newest client relationship shifted from a reactive to a predictable mode.

03 — Scope

A major UAT scope challenge converted into facts and decisions.

Situation
During UAT on a large SAP rollout, a new client-side architect requested major changes that could have broken the budget, the timeline, and team capacity.

Task
Protect the budget, timeline, and capacity while keeping the relationship constructive and every accepted change justified.

Action
Ran impact analysis request by request, separated critical from non-critical changes, anchored the discussion on the signed acceptance criteria, and negotiated formal change requests for justified paid scope.

Result
The project continued on an updated plan, the commercial position stayed healthy, and the client relationship remained constructive.

04 — Capability

Rare SAP EHS expertise turned into a repeatable practice.

Situation
Demand for SAP EHS work was growing, but the bottleneck was capability: rare specialists, a limited internal bench, and a narrow market.

Task
Turn scarce, person-dependent expertise into a repeatable, sellable team capability.

Action
Built the business case for a dedicated practice and defined roles, a hiring profile, a competency matrix, development paths, mentoring, and a knowledge-sharing model.

Result
The practice grew from 3 to 8 people, became a sellable capability, reduced dependency on isolated experts, and supported new client work.

05 — Growth

A pilot project expanded through process insight.

Situation
A focused SAP EHS pilot at a large wood-processing company revealed broader process inconsistency across its subsidiaries.

Task
Deliver the pilot and judge whether the real opportunity was larger than the original automation scope.

Action
Worked with high-level process owners and reframed the goal — the real value was process unification across subsidiaries, not automation alone.

Result
The pilot expanded into several streams; processes became more transparent, reporting became faster, and the case became a bridge into broader project leadership.
